Who Is Sean Hannity? Narrated by John Milhiser

He’s Trump’s #1 news source and everything you hate about Fox News. Here’s what you probably don't know about Sean Hannity’s cringeworthy rise to fame.

by NowThis

Updated on 10/31/2019 at 3:24PM

Sean Hannity is the host of a nationally syndicated radio show, a Fox News anchor, likely the president’s main source of news, and a “vape god.”

Sean Patrick Hannity was born in 1961 in the elite coastal city of New York. He attended a prep high school in Uniondale before attending college at NYU, UC Santa Barbara, and Adelphi University. He never graduated.

In 1989, as a young 27 year-old house painter Hannity landed his first radio gig at UC Santa Barbara’s KCSB. But he was quickly removed from the air for saying things like ”all gays are AIDS carriers,” and “Gays eat fecal matter,” and “Homosexuals are disgusting people.” 

“Anyone listening to this show that believes homosexualiy is just a normal lifestyle has been brainwashed. And you know, it’s very dangerous if we start accepting lower and lower forms of behavior as the normal,” said Hannity, on KCSB. 
When a caller who happened to be a lesbian called in, Hannity said, “I feel sorry for your child.” 

Hannity walked back his comments years later, calling them “embarrassing” and said his termination was rightful. But at the time, Hannity didn’t just step down without a fight. He reportedly tried to physically force his way back into the studio but was held back by students.

After leaving KCSB, Hannity landed back on the airwaves in Alabama and then eventually to WGST in Atlanta in 1992.

But in 1996, a new cable network was on the rise. A network that just couldn’t resist Hannity’s takes.

Hannity was drafted by Fox News in 1996 to host a program under the working title “Hannity and LTBD (liberal to be determined),” a debate show. Alan Colmes was the liberal selected and so it was: “Hannity & Colmes”.

During Hannity’s time on the program, he went off on other ventures like a nighttime radio where he could test material before bringing it to the screen, and he even started his very own dating site: “Hannidate,” where “people of like minded conservative minds can come together to meet.”

Hannity’s tinder didn’t last long, and by 2009 Alan Colmes packed up and left Hannity just as alone as the tens of Hanidate users who failed to find love on the site. This marked the end of Hannity & Colmes.

But things were looking up for Hannity who took over the time slot with his very own show creatively titled, “Hannity.”  It was on his show that Hannity ardently supported Bush during his term and attacked Obama’s birth certificate during his. And in 2012, Hannity went to bat for Romney on his radio program when a caller suggested that Romney’s wealth made him unrelatable. “When was the last time he went to bed hungry?” the caller asked. Hannity argued that poverty and hunger are not related and offered up a recipe his poor friends use.

“Friends of mine that eat rice and beans all the time. Beans protein, rice, inexpensive. You can make a big pot of this for a week, for relatively negligible amounts of money for your whole family,” said Hannity to the caller.

Around this time, Hannity was making over $10 million a year, or 12,658,228 servings of rice and beans.

2016 came and with it, the Access Hollywood tape. A tape that recorded Trump saying the infamous “grab ‘em by the p*ssy” line, which had many conservative commentators urging Trump to step down. Hannity went in a different direction. He defended Trump, saying, “King David had 500 concubines, for crying out loud!” and went on Twitter later to say it should be Chappaqua retiree Bill Clinton who should be investigated for sexual misconduct.

So when Trump seemed to be drowning, Hannity lifted him up in front of his 3 million viewers, one of whom was definitely Mr. Trump

Hannity was an early supporter of the future president and used his platform of frequent conspiracies to help him get elected.

Hannity and Trump reportedly talk after nearly every episode of Hannity, just before they go to bed.

When Hannity isn’t gabbing with the president, he’s making movies. In 2017, he executive produced “Let There Be Light,” a movie that won best picture at the “Canadian International Faith and Family Film Festival.”

But best picture at some obscure film festival focused on faith and family in Canada wasn’t the only thing Hannity had been trying for years to win. Ever since his buddy was elected president, Hannity has started peddling conspiracy theories and just about anything that would fall in line with his base to try and win over the ballot box. He pushed the theory that there’s a “deep state,” an idea that there are government agents trying to sabotage Trump and remove him from office. This is one of Alex Jones’s firm beliefs, but Hannity is taking the idea to the masses, popularizing the kind of dangerous thoughts that lead to violence and extremism.

But unlike Alex Jones who uses conspiracies to sell bogus dietary supplements and DVDs on survival, Hannity uses conspiracy methods to distract viewers from focusing too much on issues that are damaging to Trump’s character.

He’s claimed the former DNC staffer, Seth Rich was murdered by the DNC after giving emails to wikileaks. Fox News retracted the claim and Hannity went on an abrupt vacation. And when Alabama Senate candidate Roy Moore was accused of initiating sexual contact with teenagers—one of whom was 14, Hannity went on his radio program, the Sean Hannity Show, and asked, “How do you possibly tell?” On the same show, Hannity’s executive producer argued that some of the allegations were consensual. “Consensual, that’s true.” Hannity replied. Hours later after taking endless amounts of heat on Twitter, Hannity explained he had not been referring to the 14 year old, who would’ve been too young to give consent.

And then he hopped over to his Fox News program and prompted his panel with questions like, “Are there times when people make false accusations?” And “Are there times when people lie for political purposes?”

The segment made advertisers start pulling their ads from his program. Hannity’s fans got mad too: not at his cloying  attempts to cover for a pedophile, but with Keurig, for pulling its ads from Hannity’s show. Fans of Hannity posted videos of them smashing their coffee machines online and Hannity himself offered prizes for best video footage.
 
And then Hannity, while reporting the news, became the news himself, in the midst of the federal investigation of Michael Cohen, Trump’s personal lawyer.

The investigation revealed that Hannity was the infamous unnamed “third client” discussed in the investigation. Hannity said his work with Cohen was strictly limited to real estate affairs. 

The Guardian reports that those affairs involve over 870 homes owned by Hannity, purchased through shell companies that spent nearly $90 million over the past decade, with the help from loans provided by Ben Carson’s Department of Housing and Urban Development. 

Hannity who reportedly makes $36 million annually, while complaining about overpaid mainstream media elites, has a viewership of 3.3 million Americans, including the one with all the nukes, taking what he says as truth. And Sean is loving it.
